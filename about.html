Our Story

Made slowly, on purpose.

Kiln & Clay is a one-maker pottery studio in Portland, Oregon. This is the story of how it started, who's behind the wheel, and why everything here takes exactly as long as it needs to.

It started with a $60 wheel and a stubborn streak

In the winter of 2016, a secondhand potter's wheel appeared in a Craigslist listing three neighborhoods over. It wobbled, it squeaked, and it took up half the garage. It was perfect. The first few months produced mostly lopsided cylinders and a lot of reclaimed clay — but somewhere in that pile of failures was the beginning of a habit that refused to quit.

Within two years the garage had a small electric kiln, then a bigger one, then shelving in places shelving probably shouldn't go. Friends started asking to buy mugs. Then friends of friends. In 2019 we signed the lease on 412 Alder Lane, knocked out a wall, and moved the whole dusty operation into a proper studio with a showroom up front.

Today the studio fires over 1,200 pieces a year — still one wheel at a time, still glazed by hand, and still with the occasional lopsided cylinder that reminds us where we came from. That first $60 wheel? It sits by the front window. It still works. Mostly.

Wren Calloway

Founder, potter, chief kiln whisperer

"A good pot should disappear into your morning. If you notice it, it should be because it feels right in your hand."

Meet the maker

Wren came to clay the long way around — a decade in graphic design, a night class taken on a whim, and one very muddy evening that changed everything. What hooked her wasn't the finished pots. It was the wheel itself: the way clay demands your whole attention and gives you nothing until you learn to listen.

She apprenticed informally under two Oregon potters, spent a summer helping fire a wood kiln on the coast, and slowly traded pixels for porcelain. Her work leans quiet — soft profiles, honest weights, glazes pulled from the colors of the Pacific Northwest: iron red, moss, fog, and river stone.

Most days you'll find her at the wheel from early morning, trimming in the afternoon, and mixing glaze tests long after she promised herself she'd stop. Stop by the studio and she'll happily talk your ear off about shino glazes, kiln atmospheres, or why handles are the hardest part of any mug.

The Craft: From Bag of Clay to Your Cupboard

Every piece in the shop passes through the same eight hands-on stages. Start to finish, a single mug takes about three weeks. Here's where the time goes.

1

Wedging & Weighing

Each piece starts as a measured ball of stoneware or porcelain, wedged by hand to drive out air bubbles and align the clay. Skip this step and the kiln will remind you why it matters.

2

Throwing on the Wheel

The heart of the process. Every form is thrown by hand — centered, opened, and pulled up in a few focused minutes. Small variations between pieces aren't flaws; they're fingerprints.

3

Trimming & Handles

Once leather-hard, each pot is flipped and trimmed to reveal its foot. Handles are pulled from wet clay, shaped by feel, and attached one at a time — the slowest, fussiest, most rewarding part.

4

Drying, Slowly

Pieces rest under loose plastic for up to a week, drying evenly to bone-dry. Rush this stage and clay cracks. Pottery teaches patience whether you signed up for it or not.

5

Bisque Firing

The first trip through the kiln, to around 1,830°F, turns fragile greenware into porous bisque — hard enough to handle, thirsty enough to drink up glaze.

6

Glazing by Hand

We mix most of our glazes from raw materials — silica, feldspar, iron, ash. Each piece is dipped, poured, or brushed, then wiped clean at the foot so nothing sticks to the kiln shelf.

7

Glaze Firing

The final firing reaches roughly 2,230°F over twelve hours. Inside, glaze melts into glass and fuses to the clay body. What comes out is always a small surprise — that's the fire's signature.

8

Sanding & Sorting

Every piece is unloaded, sanded smooth at the foot, checked against a warm mug of tea (quality control is delicious), and shelved in the showroom — or set aside if it doesn't meet the bar.

What We Believe

Three ideas shape everything that leaves this studio.

Use it every day

We don't make pottery for glass cabinets. Every piece is food safe, dishwasher safe, and made to survive real kitchens, real mornings, and real life. Good objects earn their keep.

Honest materials

Regional clays, glazes mixed in-house from raw minerals, and no shortcuts we wouldn't be proud to explain. When you ask what something's made of, we can tell you — by the gram.

Imperfection included

A throwing line, a glaze drip caught mid-melt, a kiln kiss where two pieces sat close — these are records of the making. We leave them in. Perfect is what machines are for.

Come see the story in person

The showroom is open five days a week, and if you time it right you can watch a kiln get unloaded. Or browse what's fresh off the shelf from wherever you are.
